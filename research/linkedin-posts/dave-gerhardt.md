## Post 1: Ruthless Channel Prioritization & Audience Guardrails
- **Date Collected:** 2026-06-19
- **Source Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/davegerhardt_spoiler-alert-everything-in-marketing-works-activity-7472966743162945537-oz8X?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADsvczIBwk31Kfp-odj_kyZwsSYXsHjRRIY

### Original Content
> Spoiler alert: Everything in marketing works.
>
> I've done over 300 hours of interviews with CMOs and VPs in B2B marketing. I spent a decade as a marketing leader myself (before officially become a thought leader) and I can tell you this one thing:
>
> Everything in marketing works.
>
> YouTube works. LinkedIn works. Direct mail works. Webinars work. TikTok works. Email works. Events work. PR works. Partnerships work. Heck, even spending $75k with Gartner can work.
>
> But the challenge is figuring out which channels can work for you with the ingredients you have.
>
> You don't always have the product, the features, the timing, the wind at your back, the talent on the team, the budget. But that is the fun part about the job in marketing - you get be the chef, but sometimes there's only two eggs, a slice of bread and an onion left in the fridge. Can you work with that?
>
> The other thing that matters is the strategy. Why are you doing this. What are the goals, what is your POV on it, and how can you pull it off?
>
> Too often we try to do everything - all those channels above - because we think we have to. And the result is another B2B YouTube channel with 30 videos that all have between 17 and 62 views. You'd be better off picking one channel and doing DEEP there and then finding ways to stack those wins into other channels.
>
> This type of thinking has always been a guardrail on the teams I've been on:
>
> - What's in it for me?
> - Why should someone open this email?
> - Why would someone actually take an hour out of their day and attend your webinar? Would *you* actually go to this webinar?
> - Would you respond to that message?
> - Would you see that ad and click on it?
>
> And last thing - this is the hardest one. Often times GOOD MARKETING is not good for the company. That's right. It's harder to measure, takes time, not obvious, doesn't have UTM parameters in link; there might not be a direct response call to action. And that's what makes it good and makes it work, but you need to have the patience to trust it - and the ability to stand up in front of the CEO/CFO and articulate the marketing strategy and why you do things the way you do.
>
> What did miss?
>
> PS. If you are reading this and work in marketing, your job is to embrace this. Treat your job like a craft. Have conviction. Don't follow best practices. Use your brain. Be creative. Disrupt things inside of your company and push for a new way of doing things. More important now than ever when everyone has access to the Slop Machine ™️
>
> We write about this stuff weekly at: exitfive.com/newsletter

### AI-Native Tactical Insight
- **Core Pivot:** Rejecting multi-channel exhaustion in favor of strict single-channel dominance, guarded by hyper-empathetic audience filters and a cultural acceptance of non-linear (Dark Social) brand attribution.
- **B2B SaaS Application:** For an emerging platform like 100Hires, attempting to run a podcast, a YouTube channel, SEO, and paid ads simultaneously with a lean team guarantees failure. Gerhardt’s framework dictates a sharp operational shift:
  1. **The 100% LinkedIn Focus:** Freeze secondary, low-yield acquisition efforts and deploy all creative resources into dominating LinkedIn organic. Master this single distribution engine completely before attempting to stack or repurpose content into other ecosystems.
  2. **The Content Quality Sanity Check:** Implement Dave's internal guardrail checklist for every post, email sequence, or asset the growth team creates: *Would our ideal recruiter actually open this? Would I respond to this cold message?* If the honest answer is no, scrap the asset immediately.
  3. **Attribution Realism:** Educate company leadership that building a category-defining brand on LinkedIn won't always generate immediate click-through UTM revenue. High-value content compounds in ways that traditional software dashboards cannot track instantly; the payoff arrives later via inbound branded search and word-of-mouth pipeline.

## Post 2: B2B AI Sentiment Data (Human Taste vs. Algorithmic Slop)
- **Date Collected:** 2026-06-19
- **Source Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/davegerhardt_we-polled-540-b2b-marketers-on-how-they-feel-activity-7470420490621464576-MWC_?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADsvczIBwk31Kfp-odj_kyZwsSYXsHjRRIY

### Original Content
> We polled 540 B2B marketers on how they feel about AI right now. Here's what stood out.
>
> Note: this was just a little newsletter poll, not super scientific, but still interesting directionally if you're in B2B marketing.
>
> 1. Everyone's already using it. 
>
> 92% use AI at least weekly and 71% every single day. Whatever pushback exists isn't coming from holdouts — it's coming from people who live in these tools and have real opinions from actually using them, not from reading think pieces about them.
>
> 2. Nobody's panicking. At least they didn't admit to it.
>
> The most common feeling was "optimistic but cautious" (35%), and only about 1 in 5 rated their fear of losing their job in the next 18 months an 8 or higher out of 10. The average worry score was a 4.9 — right in the middle. There's fatigue in there, but it's hype fatigue, not tool fatigue. They've made their peace with it.
>
> 3. The fear is slop, not job loss. This is the whole "dead internet theory" prob.
>
> The # 1 worry (34%) was AI flooding every channel with low-quality content. B2B marketers aren't scared for themselves — they can see how they stay employed. 
>
> They're scared the bar is dropping while the boss asks for more AI, more efficiency, more "tell me what you did with Claude this week." That's the right thing to worry about. And it's exactly why good work is about to stand out more, not less.
>
> 4. The CMOs and VPs are the most into it using AI at work.
>
> 83% of CMOs and VPs use AI every single day, more than the directors and managers below them. They're also the least worried and, by a wide margin, the most excited. 
>
> My read: AI is handing the CMO job back to the actual craft. Less managing the machine, more taste and judgment. So the people at the top are leaning in the hardest. 
>
> Or the CMOs are thinking, sheeet I gotta learn this stuff so I don't get lapped.
>
> The full breakdown is in this week's newsletter if you're interested:
>
> https://lnkd.in/eCv-xbSV

### AI-Native Tactical Insight
- **Core Pivot:** Shifting the corporate AI directive away from mass-volume content production toward using LLMs as operational co-pilots, elevating human taste and narrative judgment as the ultimate competitive advantages.
- **B2B SaaS Application:** As an automated tool ecosystem threatens to dilute LinkedIn feeds, 100Hires can weaponize this statistical reality to win market share:
  1. **The Anti-Slop Directive:** Acknowledge that because 34% of B2B marketers are terrified of low-quality AI content flooding the market, the company's content bar must rise. Instruct the growth team to never publish raw, unedited AI drafts. AI should be used like a line cook—for rapid processing, formatting data, or building frameworks—while the final output must be heavily seasoned with real company perspective and human editing.
  2. **Executive Craft Alignment:** Lean into the data point showing that 83% of top-tier marketing executives (CMOs/VPs) use AI daily not to replace their roles, but to reclaim their craft. 100Hires' marketing leaders should use Claude/ChatGPT to accelerate administrative and systemic bottlenecks, clearing up bandwidth to focus on what AI cannot replicate: deep strategy, target audience intuition, and creative storytelling.

## Post 3: Operational ABM Capacity & The Sales Bottleneck Framework
- **Date Collected:** 2026-06-20
- **Source Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/davegerhardt_i-love-writing-about-abm-and-our-audience-activity-7468617621387636736-Z35m?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADsvczIBwk31Kfp-odj_kyZwsSYXsHjRRIY

### Original Content
> I love writing about ABM (and our audience here seems to like it too) because it is the one motion in B2B where marketing and sales actually stop arguing about credit.
>
> It's not about the credit or the score or proving what marketing did. Every other approach usually leads to someone somewhere fighting over a touch. 
>
> Did the ad influence it, did the blog post, did the SDR get there first? ABM cuts through all of that and the scoreboard gets simpler: sales picked a list of accounts, and the only question is whether marketing helped them get in or not.
>
> I recently interviewed Casey Patterson (Director of ABM at Snowflake) and pulled out for big takeaways:
>
> 1. Work backwards from sales capacity, not TAM. 
>
> Casey pointed me to The Goal by Eli Goldratt — a manufacturing book about bottlenecks. The principle: things back up at whichever stage has the least capacity. In B2B, that's almost always sales. How many reps do you have, and how much pipeline can each actually carry? Casey's team targets around ten accounts per rep when they expect to land four meetings. Sales bandwidth defines the list.
>
> 2. Run a two-week swarm on one account. 
>
> Casey's team ran a play for a single account: hands-on lab onsite, geo-fenced out-of-home ads around the location, gifting follow-up, SDR email sequence — all compressed into two weeks. They doubled Cortex users inside the account. ABM doesn't work as an always-on motion. It works as a swarm with a clear start and end date, where sales knows exactly when the wave is coming.
>
> 3. You don't need a six-figure MarTech stack. 
>
> When Casey was building ABM from scratch at her last company: 1:1 landing pages for specific accounts, direct mail with a QR code, small curated dinners around a specific pain point, and customer-to-customer introductions. No fancy tech. Just real alignment with sales on the 20 accounts that mattered most that quarter. If someone tells you otherwise, they're probably trying to sell you something.
>
> 4. Agent ABM is coming. 
>
> Casey is building toward what she calls agent ABM — stop pushing the same campaign at every account on the same schedule, start meeting buyers in the channel they prefer, with content based on what they've already engaged with. Most B2B marketing is still cable TV: same ad, same time, same channel, hope someone's tuned in. AI finally makes the personalized version possible at scale.
>
> Bonus: on finding a mentor. Casey cold-LinkedIn-messaged Hillary Carpio (now her boss at Snowflake) years before they ever worked together and turned it into a mentorship. This is right at the beginning of the conversation; so many people ask about mentorship so listen here
>
> https://lnkd.in/exCxY9RD

### AI-Native Tactical Insight
- **Core Pivot:** Shifting the B2B acquisition paradigm from infinite market targeting (TAM) to fixed sales-capacity architecture, executing high-density, time-bound "swarms" over high-value accounts without tech-stack inflation.
- **B2B SaaS Application:** For an expanding B2B SaaS platform looking to close enterprise-level accounts, spraying generic content on LinkedIn is highly inefficient. This framework outlines an operational execution pipeline:
  1. **Capacity-Driven Targeting (Takeaway 1):** Calculate the exact bandwidth of the sales team. If an Account Executive can thoroughly manage 10 target enterprise accounts per quarter, marketing must lock a tight list of only 10 high-value accounts, rather than blasting thousands of unvetted leads.
  2. **The 2-Week Omnipresent Swarm (Takeaway 2):** When targeting a major client, coordinate a synchronized, hyper-compressed 14-day campaign. While the sales development team runs targeted LinkedIn connection and messaging sequences, marketing runs hyper-targeted, localized organic content or specialized landing pages addressing that specific company's public bottlenecks. 
  3. **Low-Fi Personalization (Takeaway 3):** Bypass complex tracking software. Build simple, fast, personalized 1-to-1 pages inside the workspace or platform for those top accounts, integrating direct value propositions that answer their precise team pain points.

  ## Post 4: Agile Marketing Operations & Product-Led Team Infrastructure
- **Date Collected:** 2026-06-19
- **Source Link:** https://lnkd.in/eG_RiSET

### Original Content
> How To Run a Calm Marketing Organization (aka Why You Should Run Marketing Like The Product Team)
>
> feat. Maria Scheifler
>
> 1. Stop saying yes to everything and build a backlog.
>
> A backlog is just a prioritized list of work. Every campaign, every project, every random ask from sales sits in one place, ranked in order. 
>
> Most marketing teams operate from a mix of Slack messages, slide decks, Asana lists nobody updates, and whatever the CEO mentioned in standup last Tuesday. And then they wonder why everything feels chaotic.
>
> Maria's point: when the head of sales walks over and says "we need a one-pager for this deal by Friday," your answer isn't yes and it isn't no. It's "we'll put it on the backlog and prioritize it against everything else we have going on."
>
> 2. Plan in two-week sprints, not annual marketing plans. 
>
> The annual marketing plan is mostly a fiction we all agree to participate in. Use it as your guiding principle for goals (but beyond that it's nearly impossible to plan for). You build it in November. By February, two competitors have launched something new, you lost your top sales rep, your CEO has a fresh idea from a podcast HE NEEDS YOU TO IMPLEMENT NOW… and half the plan is irrelevant.
>
> Maria's argument: you still need a real strategy on a page. Goals, revenue targets, the big plays for the year. But the actual work should be planned in two-week chunks. Commit to what you're shipping in the next two weeks. Do a quick daily standup (10 minutes, what I did yesterday, what I'm doing today, what's blocked). Review the work at the end of the sprint and invite stakeholders in. Then do a retro.
>
> She told me about running sprint reviews at her last public company. Twelve people, one hour, everyone presents what they shipped. Recorded. Shared across the org. Suddenly the "what does marketing actually do?" question stops getting asked. The receipts are right there in Slack.
>
> 3. Start finishing, stop starting.
>
> Most marketing teams have 8, 10, 12 projects in motion at any given time. None close to done. Everyone is busy. Nothing ships. Or the stuff that ships doesn't move the needle.
>
> Maria's lesson: start finishing things before you start new things. Kinda like how I make my kids clean up the kitchen before starting a new activity in the playroom.
>
> Example: right now at Exit Five, our big campaign is selling tickets to Drive 2026. There's a ton of work that goes into that and the instinct is to wait until it's all ready before launching. Instead: what's the minimum viable thing you can ship right now that delivers value? We've sold 200 tickets and the full speaker lineup isn't even announced yet. Shrink it to the smallest thing you could ship and start getting feedback out in the wild.
>
>If you work in B2B marketing and want a resource to help you with your job that's not an AI, get our weekly newsletter here:
>
> https://lnkd.in/eG_RiSET

### AI-Native Tactical Insight
- **Core Pivot:** Transitioning marketing operations from a chaotic, reactive corporate service-desk into a highly disciplined, product-led engineering motion governed by a single prioritized backlog and bi-weekly sprint delivery metrics.
- **B2B SaaS Application:** For a high-velocity platform like 100Hires, content production and campaign execution frequently bottleneck due to changing internal priorities. Implementing this Agile infrastructure establishes immediate operational sanity:
  1. **The Backlog Shield (Point 1):** Centralize all inbound marketing tasks, content ideas, and cross-department requests (like Sales demanding a new PDF collateral) into a single, ranked backlog. When Slack requests fly in, the growth team's response shifts from an immediate "yes" to an objective "we will queue it in the backlog and prioritize its impact against our core LinkedIn acquisition targets."
  2. **Bi-Weekly Content Sprints (Point 2):** Abolish rigid, long-term content calendars that decay within months. Plan content distribution in rigid 14-day execution chunks. Run 10-minute daily standups to diagnose creative blocks or algorithmic friction, and review shipped metrics at the end of the sprint to transparently demonstrate marketing's concrete pipeline velocity to leadership.
  3. **The Micro-Campaign MVP (Point 3):** Eradicate the instinct to delay campaigns until "everything is perfect." If the team is launching an educational narrative or product feature sequence, ship the minimum viable asset directly to the LinkedIn feed to gather immediate audience interaction, iterating the campaign trajectory based on live, data-backed feedback.

## Post 5: The Brand Moat Against AI-Agent Commoditization
- **Date Collected:** 2026-06-19
- **Source Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/davegerhardt_brand-is-all-that-matters-right-now-one-activity-7465712716054343680-xuD9?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADsvczIBwk31Kfp-odj_kyZwsSYXsHjRRIY

### Original Content
> Brand is all that matters right now. 
>
> One of the cool things about my job as the owner of Exit Five is I get to talk to CMOs and marketing leaders every week. 
>
> And the one place they are investing (and thinking deeply about) that’s not AI is BRAND. 
>
> This is: creative, events, mail, swag, out of home, and digging deep to re-connect with customers. 
>
> Because in a world where my AI agent is doing all of the research for buying things and presenting me a list of the top 2-3 vendors to pick, the last great differentiator just might be brand. 
>
> I picked them because well, I dunno. The AI said they have all the features I want. But I met their team at this event and they had awesome swag. It was a Nike golf hat that I’m actually going to wear. 
>
> Or the founder is actually a good person and kind and funny and authentic.
>
> Some might call it taste, but that’s incredibly hard to define and we all have different taste. To some, the Olive Garden is the best Italian restaurant in the country. But if I ever brought Matthew Carnevale a bag of leftovers from the Garden I think he resign on the spot. 
>
> You know what I’m saying here?
>
> Might be time to dust off the ol Guerilla Marketing playbook. 
>
> Stand out. Be different. Find new ways to connect with customers beyond “but did we show up in Perplexity search???”

### AI-Native Tactical Insight
- **Core Pivot:** Shifting corporate positioning from objective feature-and-attribute optimization to deep human-to-human resonance and creative distinctiveness, neutralizing a future where AI procurement agents commoditize vendor selection based purely on functional checklist matching.
- **B2B SaaS Application:** When prospective enterprise buyers rely on AI search engines (like Perplexity or ChatGPT) to evaluate recruitment and HR platforms, software capabilities become instantly flattened. 100Hires can deploy Dave's brand playbook to survive the filtering layer:
  1. **The Executive/Founder Trust Factor:** Humanize the company's digital presence by empowering leadership to post raw, distinct, and even humorous points of view on LinkedIn. When automated product descriptions look identical, decision-makers default to purchasing from founders who demonstrate high empathy, character, and authentic authority in the feed.
  2. **Guerilla Memorability:** Supplement programmatic distribution with unforgettable, platform-native creative touchpoints. Deliver high-utility, un-gated content directly to the target network so that when a procurement cycle opens, the buyer explicitly instructs their team (or their AI agent) to evaluate 100Hires by name, completely bypassing the automated discovery funnel.
